Caddie Tips

First Tee Advice

Based upon your Golf Range practise I advise that you command no more than 170-yards, straight down the fairway.

However trying to hit 170-yards with your handicap and the OmniAtlas starter driver comes with high risks, including out of bounds.

Your safer option may be to command a maximum of 130-yards straight down the fairway.

You may even reduce your command further, in exchange for a higher likelihood of accuracy.

First Fairway Shot Advice

Based upon your Golfing Range practise, using this OmniAtlas Starter driver from the fairway, I strongly advise that you command no more than 150-yards with your handicap.

A 150-yard fairway driver is possible, but the odds should be extremely stacked against it and heavily in favour of a severe mishit.

Perhaps you should consider making a safer command, such as 110-yards, meaning a greater chance of accuracy.

You might also consider using a 3-wood, or 5-wood, or even an iron, for less risky results.

36-Handicapper Driver Profile — Parkland Tee Shot Logic

This player profile represents a 36-handicap golfer using the OmniAtlas Virtual Reality Steel 4 Driver from the tee on a parkland course.

Average driving distance is approximately 119–130 yards total, with an average carry distance of 110–120 yards. On parkland courses, softer fairways reduce roll, so total distance tends to settle closer to the lower end of the range.

While this golfer may occasionally hit a strong drive of 160+ yards, those longer results are not the normal playing average. Mishits, weak strikes, and directional misses reduce real on-course consistency.

36-Handicapper Driver Profile — Parkland Fairway Shot Logic

An average golfer with a 36-handicap using the OmniAtlas starter driver would typically hit the driver from the fairway between 110 and 120 yards.

While a 36-handicap player using the OmniAtlas starter driver might extremely rarely hit the ball 150–170 yards from a tee, hitting driver from the fairway generally produces significantly less efficient results because of lower launch, reduced strike quality, and less roll.

On a parkland course, this player runs a substantial risk of hitting the ground first. A heavy mishit can reduce distance by approximately 50 yards compared with the average outcome.

On flatter, well-maintained parkland ground, the player is more realistically trying to advance the ball with solid contact rather than maximise distance.
